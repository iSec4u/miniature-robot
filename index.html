Red Flags: How to Spot Phishing

Learn to recognize these common warning signs in emails and messages

📧

Suspicious Sender Address

The email address doesn't match the company name or contains misspellings.

  • Look for extra characters or numbers
  • Check the domain after the @ symbol
  • Verify it matches the official company website
  • Example: "microsoft-security@support.com" is fake
⏰

Urgency and Pressure

Creates panic with threats of account suspension, legal action, or urgent deadlines.

  • "Act now or lose access!"
  • "Your account will be closed in 24 hours"
  • "Immediate action required"
  • Take time to verify - legitimate companies won't rush you
🔗

Suspicious Links

Hover over links to see where they really go before clicking.

  • URL doesn't match the displayed text
  • Strange domain names with odd characters
  • Shortened URLs hiding the real destination
  • When in doubt, go directly to the website
📝

Poor Grammar & Formatting

Professional companies proofread their communications carefully.

  • Spelling and grammatical errors
  • Awkward phrasing or translation issues
  • Inconsistent formatting and fonts
  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer"
🔐

Requests for Sensitive Info

Legitimate companies NEVER ask for passwords or financial info via email.

  • Asking for passwords or PINs
  • Requesting credit card numbers
  • Social Security numbers
  • Security question answers
📎

Unexpected Attachments

Attachments can contain malware - only open if you're expecting them.

  • .exe, .zip, or .scr files
  • Attachments from unknown senders
  • Files with double extensions (.pdf.exe)
  • Macro-enabled documents

Anatomy of the Phishing Email

Let's break down what made this email suspicious

⚠️ Phishing Email Analysis
From:
"Microsoft Security" <microsoft-security@support.com> 🚩 RED FLAG
Display Name:
Microsoft Security (looks official, but it's fake)
Actual Domain:
notice.com (NOT from Microsoft or your organization)
Subject:
Ticket [AF270438] opened Password Reset 16_30 PM 06 Mar 2026 Expires Today!!! 🚩 URGENCY
To:
Steve

🔍 Key Indicators This Was Phishing:

  • Sender Mismatch: Display name says "Microsoft Security" but email is from "notice.com"
  • Domain Red Flag: Real Microsoft emails come from @microsoft.com domains
  • Urgency Tactic: "Expires today" creates false sense of emergency
  • Generic Content: Real IT would use specific organizational language and procedures

What To Do If You Receive a Suspicious Email

Follow these steps to protect yourself and your organization

1

Don't Click Anything

Stop! Don't click any links, download attachments, or reply to the email. Even hovering over links can trigger some attacks.

2

Verify Through Another Channel

If the email claims to be from your bank, IT department, or a service you use, contact them directly using a phone number or website you know is legitimate - NOT from the email.

3

Report to IT Security

Forward the suspicious email to your IT security team or use your organization's phishing report button. This helps protect your colleagues and improves security filters.

4

Delete the Email

After reporting, delete the email from your inbox and trash folder. Don't keep it around "just in case" - that's what backups are for.

5

If You Already Clicked...

Don't panic! Immediately notify IT security via infosec.incidents@slkgroup.com, change your password on a different device, and monitor your accounts for suspicious activity. The faster you report it, the better.

Best Practices for Email Security

Build these habits to stay protected

Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA adds an extra layer of security beyond just your password. Even if attackers get your password, they can't access your account without the second factor.

Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Use a password manager to create and store complex passwords. Never reuse passwords across different accounts.

Keep Software Updated

Regular updates patch security vulnerabilities. Enable automatic updates for your operating system, browser, and applications.

Think Before You Click

Take a moment to evaluate emails before clicking. If something feels off, it probably is. Trust your instincts.

Verify Sender Identity

Check the full email address, not just the display name. When in doubt, verify through an independent channel.

Be Cautious with Personal Information

Limit what you share on social media. Attackers use publicly available information to make phishing attempts more convincing.

Use Company VPN on Public WiFi

Public networks are less secure. Always use your organization's VPN when working remotely or on public WiFi.

Regular Security Training

Stay informed about the latest phishing tactics. Attend security awareness training and read security updates from your IT team.
